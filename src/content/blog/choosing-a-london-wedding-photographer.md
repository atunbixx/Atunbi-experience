There are roughly four thousand wedding photographers within the M25. Choosing one is harder than it should be, mostly because the language they use is the same — "documentary", "editorial", "candid" — and the prices stretch from £400 to £40,000. This guide is for the kind of couple who care less about the photographer's brand and more about whether the pictures will still look right in a decade.

## Five questions worth asking

### 1. Show me a full gallery, not a highlight reel

Anyone can curate twelve good frames. Ask to see *everything* from one wedding — 300+ images. The shape of the day, the dull middle hours, the difficult light at the speeches. That's where the standard lives.

### 2. Who edits the pictures?

If the photographer doesn't, you'll get lab-edits or AI-presets. Ask, point-blank: "do you finish every image yourself?" The right answer is yes, and they should be willing to explain their process.

### 3. What happens if it rains in Mayfair?

The answer should be specific to your venue, not generic. Anyone can quote you "we've shot in worse"; you want "I know the covered colonnade at Claridge's, and we'd move the group portraits there by 4pm".

### 4. How many weddings have you covered at *this* venue?

There's a difference between a great photographer and a great photographer at *your* venue. Familiarity is a quiet kind of insurance.

### 5. Can I speak to a recent couple?

A real one, ideally from this year. Forty-five minutes with someone who's been through it tells you more than ten Instagram reels.

## Three questions that don't matter

- **"What camera do you use?"** Anyone competent has good kit. The kit isn't the work.
- **"Do you offer raw files?"** Most don't, and you don't want them. Raw files are unedited drafts.
- **"Will you stay until the last dance?"** This is a coverage question, not a quality one — agree the hours up front, then trust them.

## On pricing

A full-day London wedding from a photographer who edits their own work, scouts the venue, and delivers a finished gallery starts around £1,800–£2,500. Below that, something is being cut: the edit, the time, or the experience. Above £4,000, you're paying for brand, not pictures.
